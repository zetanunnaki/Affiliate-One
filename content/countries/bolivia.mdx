## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Bolivia scores 56 out of 100 on the Freedom House Freedom on the Net index, classifying it as "Partly Free." While outright censorship is uncommon, the government has passed legislation that could enable surveillance and there have been documented cases of social media monitoring during periods of political unrest. The country's telecommunications market is served by three major operators: Entel (state-owned), Tigo (Millicom), and Viva (Nuevatel). Entel dominates mobile connectivity nationwide, while Tigo and Viva compete strongly in urban centers like La Paz, Cochabamba, and Santa Cruz. Fixed broadband penetration remains relatively low, with most Bolivians accessing the internet primarily through mobile data connections.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are legal in Bolivia. There are no restrictions on VPN usage, and both personal and commercial use is permitted without legal consequences.

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Bolivia does not have in-country VPN servers from most major providers. The closest server locations include Brazil (Sao Paulo), Chile (Santiago), Peru (Lima), and Argentina (Buenos Aires). Lima and Santiago servers typically offer the lowest latency for Bolivian users, with ping times of 50-80ms from urban areas. Users on Tigo fiber in Santa Cruz or La Paz can expect VPN speeds of 20-50 Mbps, while mobile connections through Entel's 4G network will deliver lower throughput, especially at Bolivia's higher altitude cities where infrastructure is more challenged.

## Best VPN Protocols for Bolivia

WireGuard is the optimal choice for Bolivia's variable bandwidth conditions, significantly outperforming OpenVPN on mobile data connections that are common throughout the country. For users experiencing intermittent connectivity in highland or rural areas, WireGuard's ability to quickly re-establish connections after drops is particularly valuable. OpenVPN (TCP mode) serves as a reliable fallback when network conditions are unstable.
