## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Bosnia and Herzegovina is not formally assessed by Freedom House's Freedom on the Net report, but the country maintains a generally open internet without systematic censorship. The telecommunications market is divided along entity lines, with BH Telecom serving the Federation, HT Eronet (owned by HT Group/Deutsche Telekom) covering Croat-majority areas, and m:tel (Telekom Srbija subsidiary) operating in Republika Srpska. This fragmented market structure means regulation varies somewhat between entities, and data protection legislation lags behind EU standards despite the country's EU accession aspirations. The Personal Data Protection Law (2006) is outdated and predates modern surveillance capabilities, and the Communications Regulatory Agency (RAK) has limited cross-entity enforcement power. Bosnia received EU candidate status in 2022, but harmonizing privacy regulations across both entities remains a major accession challenge.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are fully legal in Bosnia and Herzegovina with no restrictions on use. The country's regulatory framework does not prohibit encrypted communications, and VPN traffic is neither blocked nor throttled by any of the three major ISPs.

## Why You Need a VPN in Bosnia and Herzegovina

Bosnia's affordable internet and growing tech scene in Sarajevo attract remote workers and entrepreneurs, but the fragmented regulatory environment and lack of comprehensive data protection make VPN use prudent for anyone handling sensitive information.

- **Fragmented data protection laws** — Bosnia has not yet aligned its data protection framework with GDPR, meaning BH Telecom, HT Eronet, and m:tel operate under less stringent privacy obligations than EU-based ISPs; a VPN prevents any provider from logging your browsing activity
- **Public Wi-Fi across Sarajevo and Mostar** — The growing cafe culture and co-working scene means frequent use of shared networks in Old Town Sarajevo, Bascarsija district, and Mostar tourist areas where traffic interception is trivially easy without encryption
- **Access to geo-restricted EU services** — Many Bosnians work remotely for EU companies and need access to services that restrict non-EU IP addresses; connecting to a nearby EU server solves this instantly
- **ISP throttling during peak hours** — BH Telecom and m:tel have been reported throttling streaming and torrent traffic during evening peaks (7-11 PM); a VPN masks traffic type and eliminates protocol-based throttling

## Best VPN Protocols for Bosnia and Herzegovina

WireGuard is the recommended protocol for Bosnia, delivering peak performance on the country's increasingly fiber-connected urban networks. BH Telecom's fiber plans support speeds up to 1 Gbps in Sarajevo, and WireGuard's minimal overhead preserves as much of that bandwidth as possible. OpenVPN (UDP) works well as a fallback. Bosnia does not employ DPI or VPN-blocking technology on any of its networks.
