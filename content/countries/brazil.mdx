## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Brazil scores 64 out of 100 on the Freedom House Freedom on the Net index, placing it in the "Partly Free" category. While internet access is widespread — over 180 million Brazilians are online — the country faces a complex landscape of judicial content blocking, platform shutdowns, and elevated cybercrime. Major ISPs including Vivo (Telefonica Brasil), Claro (America Movil), TIM Brasil, and Oi provide broadband coverage, though quality varies significantly between wealthy urban centers and rural or favela areas.

Brazil's Marco Civil da Internet (2014) established net neutrality and privacy principles, but subsequent judicial actions have repeatedly undermined these protections. Courts have ordered temporary blocks of WhatsApp (multiple times between 2015-2016), Telegram (2023), and X/Twitter (2024), affecting hundreds of millions of users. The Lei Geral de Protecao de Dados (LGPD) provides GDPR-like protections on paper, but the ANPD's enforcement capacity remains limited. Brazilian ISPs collect extensive connection metadata, and law enforcement agencies access this data through relatively permissive judicial processes. The country consistently ranks among the top three globally for phishing attacks, banking trojans, and online fraud.

## VPN Legality

VPN usage is fully legal in Brazil. The Marco Civil da Internet protects the right to use encryption tools, and no Brazilian legislation restricts VPN services. Citizens can freely download and use any VPN provider. During platform blocking events (such as the 2024 X/Twitter shutdown ordered by the Supreme Tribunal Federal), VPN usage surged by over 1,500% according to industry data — and while some authorities expressed displeasure, no legal action was taken against individual VPN users. Brazilian law does not require VPN providers to register or maintain local servers, making offshore providers freely accessible.

## Why You Need a VPN in Brazil

- **Judicial platform blocking** — Brazilian courts routinely order ISP-level blocks of major platforms (WhatsApp, Telegram, X/Twitter) during political disputes; a VPN ensures uninterrupted access to communication tools
- **Extreme cybercrime exposure** — Brazil ranks among the top three countries globally for phishing, banking trojans (Grandoreiro, Mekotio, Casbaneiro), and credential theft; VPN encryption protects against man-in-the-middle attacks on shared networks
- **Public Wi-Fi risks** — Airports (Guarulhos, Galeao, Congonhas, Brasilia), shopping malls, bus terminals, and Sao Paulo/Rio metro systems offer open Wi-Fi with minimal security, heavily targeted by local cybercriminals
- **Pix payment protection** — Over 150 million Brazilians use Pix instant payments; criminals exploit public Wi-Fi to intercept banking sessions and redirect transactions via network-level attacks
- **ISP monitoring and throttling** — Vivo, Claro, TIM Brasil, and Oi collect extensive connection data and have been caught throttling streaming services despite net neutrality provisions
- **Political surveillance concerns** — Brazilian intelligence agencies (ABIN) have expanded digital surveillance capabilities; journalists, activists, and political figures face targeted monitoring

## Best VPN Protocols for Brazil

Brazil does not deploy deep packet inspection or protocol-level VPN blocking, so all standard protocols function normally. WireGuard is recommended as the primary protocol for maximum speed on Brazilian fiber connections, which have improved dramatically in recent years but still benefit from minimal VPN overhead. OpenVPN (UDP) provides the most reliable fallback on inconsistent mobile networks from Vivo, Claro, and TIM. During platform blocking events, Brazilian ISPs typically implement DNS-based and IP-based blocking — WireGuard and OpenVPN both bypass these methods effectively. For users on slower connections in northern or rural areas, WireGuard's lightweight design minimizes the performance impact that heavier protocols would impose.
