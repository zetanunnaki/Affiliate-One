## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Burundi scores approximately 18 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it firmly in the "Not Free" category. The government maintains tight control over the digital space, with documented social media shutdowns during elections and political crises — most notably during the 2015 political crisis and subsequent election periods. Econet Leo (Lumitel), LACELL (Smart), and Onatel (state-owned) are the primary mobile operators, while CBINET provides fixed broadband. Internet penetration remains under 15%, concentrated in the capital Bujumbura, with most access occurring through mobile data on 3G and limited 4G networks. The National Intelligence Service (SNR) conducts surveillance of online activities, and journalists and activists have been arrested based on social media posts. The government requires SIM card registration linked to national identity.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are not explicitly banned by Burundian law, but the government has taken steps to restrict their use during political crises. During the 2015 shutdown and subsequent events, authorities attempted to block VPN protocols. In practice, VPN use is common among civil society, journalists, and the diaspora community, though using circumvention tools to access blocked content carries implicit risk of government attention.

## Why You Need a VPN in Burundi

Burundi's history of political censorship and surveillance makes VPN protection essential for anyone concerned about privacy and access.

- **Bypass election-period shutdowns** — The government has repeatedly blocked social media during elections and political tensions; VPN connections circumvent these blocks to maintain communications
- **Protect against SNR surveillance** — National intelligence monitors online communications and has used intercepted messages as grounds for arrest; VPN encryption prevents traffic interception at the ISP level
- **Access blocked news and opposition content** — Independent media websites, diaspora news platforms, and opposition social media accounts face persistent blocking; VPN tunnels provide unrestricted access to information

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No VPN provider operates servers within Burundi. The nearest locations are in Kenya (Nairobi), Tanzania (Dar es Salaam), and South Africa (Johannesburg). Burundi is landlocked and connects internationally through terrestrial fiber links to Tanzania and Kenya. Baseline speeds in Bujumbura average 2-8 Mbps on 4G mobile connections, with fixed broadband limited to select business areas. VPN connections to Kenyan servers add 40-70ms latency with 25-40% throughput reduction. Tanzanian servers may offer slightly lower latency at 30-50ms. South African servers deliver 80-130ms. The limited bandwidth means VPN-connected speeds are typically suitable for messaging, social media, and basic browsing rather than high-bandwidth applications.

## Best VPN Protocols for Burundi

During political crises, Burundian authorities deploy VPN blocking at the ISP level, targeting common protocols. Standard OpenVPN and WireGuard face interference during shutdown events. Obfuscated protocols are essential for reliable access during sensitive periods. NordVPN's obfuscated servers, Surfshark's Camouflage Mode, and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol disguise VPN traffic as normal HTTPS. Users should maintain both standard configurations (for normal use) and pre-configured obfuscated options for crisis periods.
