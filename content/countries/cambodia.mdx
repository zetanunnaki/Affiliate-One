## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Cambodia scores 25 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as "Not Free." Under Prime Minister Hun Manet (succeeding his father Hun Sen), the government maintains strict control over online expression. The 2021 National Internet Gateway (NIG) law gives authorities centralized control over all internet traffic entering and leaving the country, enabling real-time monitoring and content blocking. Independent media outlets have been forced to close, and citizens have been arrested for Facebook posts critical of the government.

The telecommunications market is served by major operators including Smart (Axiata Group), Metfone (Viettel), and Cellcard (Royal Group). These providers offer 4G coverage in Phnom Penh and Siem Reap with expanding rural reach. Fixed broadband from providers like EZECOM, OpenNet, and SINET delivers 30-100 Mbps in Phnom Penh's central areas. Internet costs remain among the cheapest in Southeast Asia at $15-30/month for home broadband. The USD-based economy simplifies subscription payments for foreign visitors and residents.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is technically legal in Cambodia, but the 2021 National Internet Gateway law grants the government broad powers to block or restrict any internet traffic, including VPN protocols. While enforcement against individual VPN users has not been widespread, the legal framework allows for restriction at any time. The government has blocked opposition websites and could extend blocking to VPN services during politically sensitive periods. Install and configure VPN software before relying on it.

## Why You Need a VPN in Cambodia

Cambodia's "Not Free" internet classification combined with the National Internet Gateway makes VPN critical for both privacy and access.

- **Evade National Internet Gateway surveillance** — The NIG routes all international traffic through government-controlled chokepoints enabling real-time content inspection; VPN encryption prevents authorities from reading your communications, browsing history, and financial transactions
- **Access blocked independent media** — The government has shut down numerous independent news outlets (Cambodia Daily, Voice of Democracy) and blocks their websites; a VPN provides access to uncensored reporting about Cambodia from diaspora and international media
- **Protect digital nomad work on shared networks** — Phnom Penh and Siem Reap's cafe culture means constant use of shared Wi-Fi that passes through monitored infrastructure; sensitive client work, banking, and personal communications require VPN encryption

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No VPN providers operate physical servers in Cambodia. The nearest server locations are in Singapore, Vietnam (Ho Chi Minh City), Thailand (Bangkok), and Hong Kong. Cambodia's internet routes primarily through Vietnam (via Metfone/Viettel's infrastructure) and Singapore submarine cables. Expect latency of 30-50 ms to Singapore, 20-40 ms to Vietnamese servers, and 25-45 ms to Bangkok. With Phnom Penh's baseline of 30-100 Mbps, VPN connections through Singapore typically retain 70-85% of base speed. In Siem Reap, where baseline speeds are lower (15-50 Mbps), VPN overhead is more noticeable but adequate for remote work and streaming.

## Best VPN Protocols for Cambodia

WireGuard is the recommended protocol for Cambodia under current conditions, maximizing speed on moderate bandwidth connections. However, the National Internet Gateway's DPI capabilities mean obfuscation should be configured as a fallback. OpenVPN over TCP port 443 provides reliable camouflage as standard HTTPS traffic if the government begins actively blocking VPN protocols. Given Cambodia's political trajectory, having Shadowsocks or V2Ray alternatives pre-configured is prudent preparation for potential escalation of restrictions.
