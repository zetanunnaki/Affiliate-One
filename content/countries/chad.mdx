## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Chad scores approximately 28 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it firmly in the "Not Free" category. The government has a well-documented history of imposing lengthy internet shutdowns during political crises — most notoriously a 16-month social media ban from 2018-2019, among the longest in African history. Tigo Chad (now operating under Moov Africa) and Airtel Chad are the primary mobile operators, while Sotel Tchad provides limited fixed-line services. Internet penetration remains under 15%, concentrated in the capital N'Djamena, with vast Saharan regions entirely unconnected. The government routinely orders social media blocks and complete shutdowns surrounding elections, protests, and military operations. SudaChad Telecom operates the international gateway, giving authorities centralized control over all international traffic.

## VPN Legality

There is no specific law banning VPNs in Chad, but authorities have actively attempted to block VPN services during shutdown periods. The government's telecommunications regulator ARCEP has directed ISPs to implement VPN blocking during political crises. In practice, VPN use is widespread among educated urban populations, journalists, and civil society, but users should be prepared for periods when standard VPN connections are deliberately blocked.

## Why You Need a VPN in Chad

Chad's history of extended shutdowns and social media bans makes a VPN essential for maintaining communications during crises.

- **Survive social media bans** — The government regularly orders blocks on WhatsApp, Facebook, and Twitter during political tensions; VPN connections bypass these blocks through encrypted tunnels that ISPs cannot selectively filter
- **Maintain communications during shutdowns** — During partial shutdowns that throttle specific services rather than killing all connectivity, VPN encryption defeats protocol-specific blocking
- **Protect against surveillance** — Security services monitor online communications, particularly targeting journalists, opposition members, and civil society activists; VPN encryption prevents traffic interception

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No VPN provider operates servers within Chad. The nearest locations are in Cameroon, Nigeria, and France (the former colonial power whose submarine cables serve the region). Chad is landlocked and relies entirely on terrestrial fiber connections through Cameroon to reach submarine cables. Baseline speeds in N'Djamena average 3-10 Mbps on 4G mobile connections, with fixed-line services rarely exceeding 5 Mbps. VPN connections to French servers add 100-150ms latency with 25-40% throughput reduction. Nigerian servers may offer lower latency at 60-100ms. During government-imposed throttling, effective speeds drop regardless of VPN usage, though encrypted tunnels can sometimes bypass protocol-specific restrictions.

## Best VPN Protocols for Chad

During Chad's frequent censorship episodes, authorities deploy DPI and VPN blocking technologies at the international gateway level. Standard OpenVPN and WireGuard are targeted during shutdowns. Obfuscated protocols are essential for reliable access during political crises. NordVPN's obfuscated servers, Surfshark's Camouflage Mode, and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol disguise traffic as regular HTTPS, bypassing blocks. Users should maintain both standard and obfuscated configurations — standard for normal periods (better speed), obfuscated for crisis periods (reliable access).
