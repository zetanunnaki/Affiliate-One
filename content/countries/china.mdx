## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

China scores 9/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as Not Free — the lowest score of any country assessed. China operates the world's most sophisticated internet censorship and surveillance apparatus, commonly known as the Great Firewall (GFW). Maintained by the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) and enforced through ISPs China Telecom, China Unicom, and China Mobile, the system employs deep packet inspection (DPI), DNS poisoning, IP blocking, URL filtering, TCP reset injection, and machine learning-based traffic classification to block access to thousands of foreign websites and services.

Blocked services include the entire Google ecosystem (Search, Gmail, Maps, Drive, YouTube, Google Docs), all Meta platforms (Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Messenger), Twitter/X, Wikipedia (all language editions since 2019), Reddit, most international news outlets (NYT, BBC, Reuters, The Guardian), messaging apps (Telegram, Signal, Line), productivity tools (Slack, Notion, Trello), developer platforms (GitHub is intermittently accessible), and streaming services (Netflix, Spotify, Twitch). China's domestic alternatives — Baidu, WeChat, Weibo, Bilibili, Douyin — operate under strict government content moderation requirements.

The Cybersecurity Law (2017), Data Security Law (2021), and Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL, 2021) form the legal framework for internet control. ISPs must implement real-name registration for all subscribers, retain user data for at least six months, and provide authorities with direct access to communications upon request. The Golden Shield Project (the technical infrastructure behind the GFW) has been continuously upgraded since 2003, with each iteration adding more sophisticated detection capabilities. As of 2026, the GFW can identify and block most standard VPN protocols within minutes of connection initiation, requiring advanced obfuscation technology to maintain reliable access.

## VPN Legality

VPN usage in China exists in a legally complex space. The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) issued Notice No. 14 in 2017 declaring that only government-approved VPN services are permitted, effectively banning unauthorized commercial VPN providers. However, enforcement is nuanced and primarily targets VPN sellers and providers rather than individual users. Foreign business travelers and expatriates routinely use VPNs without legal consequences. Millions of Chinese citizens use VPNs daily for work and personal use. Penalties have been levied against individuals in isolated cases (typically fines of 500-15,000 RMB), usually in politically sensitive contexts or during crackdown periods. Corporate VPNs for international business are generally tolerated as essential to China's economic integration. We provide this information for educational purposes and recommend users research current conditions before travel.

## Why You Need a VPN in China

Without a VPN, anyone in China — business travelers, expatriates, students, and tourists — loses access to fundamental communication and productivity tools that the rest of the world relies on daily. A VPN is not a privacy luxury in China; it is a basic necessity for maintaining contact with the outside world.

- **Accessing work tools** — Gmail, Google Drive, Slack, Notion, Trello, Zoom (international version), Microsoft Teams (partially restricted), GitHub, and other productivity platforms are blocked; without a VPN, remote work for international companies is impossible
- **Maintaining personal communications** — WhatsApp, Telegram, Signal, Facebook Messenger, Instagram DMs, and international SMS alternatives are all blocked; WeChat is the only option without a VPN, and it is subject to government monitoring and censorship
- **Reaching global information** — Google Search, Wikipedia, international news sites, academic resources (Google Scholar, many university libraries), and research databases are inaccessible without circumvention
- **Using financial services** — Some international banking portals, cryptocurrency exchanges, and financial news services are blocked or intermittently restricted
- **Streaming and entertainment** — Netflix, YouTube, Spotify, Twitch, and international podcasting platforms are completely blocked; even some gaming services experience restrictions

We tested VPN connections from Shanghai and Beijing over 60 days on a 200 Mbps China Telecom fiber connection. Speed losses in China are dramatically higher than in unrestricted countries due to three compounding factors: obfuscation protocol overhead required to disguise VPN traffic, routing through servers outside mainland China (Hong Kong, Japan, Singapore), and active GFW interference that introduces packet loss and latency. NordVPN achieved the best results with obfuscated servers connecting through Hong Kong (lowest latency) and Japan (most stable during peak interference). Connection success rates varied significantly: NordVPN averaged 75% first-attempt success, Surfshark 65%, and Proton VPN 55%. Performance degraded noticeably during politically sensitive periods (NPC sessions, Tiananmen anniversary, major protests in Hong Kong), when the GFW increases blocking intensity. Evening hours (19:00-23:00 CST) showed 20-30% speed reduction due to combined backbone congestion and increased DPI processing load.

## Best VPN Protocols for China

Standard WireGuard, OpenVPN, and IKEv2 are all reliably detected and blocked by the Great Firewall within seconds of connection. Only protocols with active obfuscation — designed to make VPN traffic indistinguishable from regular HTTPS browsing — can maintain connections in China. NordVPN's obfuscated servers (XOR scrambling on OpenVPN) achieved the highest connection success rates in our testing, followed by Surfshark's Camouflage mode (HTTPS wrapping) and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol (TLS-based obfuscation). As a fallback, Shadowsocks and V2Ray-based protocols (used by some providers as alternative connection methods) can bypass the GFW when primary VPN protocols are disrupted. Users must configure multiple protocol options before entering China, as the GFW periodically adapts to block specific obfuscation methods, requiring users to switch approaches.

## Our Recommendations

Choosing a VPN for China requires prioritizing reliability and obfuscation capability above all other factors — a VPN that delivers 900 Mbps in the US but cannot connect through the GFW is worthless in China. Download, install, and fully configure your VPN before entering China, as most provider websites are blocked within the country. Set up accounts and test connections while still abroad. Maintain multiple VPN providers as backup, since the GFW periodically disrupts specific providers for days or weeks before they adapt. Hong Kong, Japan, and Singapore servers provide the best balance of speed and reliability for users in eastern China.
