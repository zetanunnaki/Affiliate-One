## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Croatia scores 64/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it in the "Free" category but notably lower than its EU neighbors Slovenia and Austria. The country benefits from EU membership and GDPR protections, which provide a solid legal framework for data privacy. However, concerns remain about government surveillance capabilities and data retention requirements imposed on telecommunications providers under Croatian law.

Local ISPs: A1 Hrvatska (formerly VIPnet), Hrvatski Telekom (T-Com), and Telemach (formerly Tele2) dominate the market. These providers are required to retain metadata for up to 12 months under Croatian telecommunications regulations, and there have been documented cases of law enforcement accessing browsing records without judicial oversight in national security investigations. The Croatian Personal Data Protection Agency (AZOP) has limited resources to enforce compliance, and a 2025 report revealed that Hrvatski Telekom processed over 5,000 law enforcement data requests in a single year — the highest per-capita rate among EU Adriatic nations.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are fully legal in Croatia. As an EU member state, Croatia recognizes the right to use encryption and privacy tools without restriction. There are no laws prohibiting VPN usage for personal or commercial purposes, and the government does not block VPN protocols or services.

## Why You Need a VPN in Croatia

Croatia adopted the Euro in 2023 and joined the Schengen Area, attracting digital nomads and remote workers to its Adriatic coastline. Whether you are working from a cafe in Split or connecting through hotel Wi-Fi in Dubrovnik, a VPN remains essential for maintaining privacy and accessing geo-restricted content.

- **Accessing geo-restricted content** — Croatian IP addresses cannot access certain US and UK streaming libraries. Remote workers also need VPNs to reach corporate resources locked to home-country IPs. Croatian broadcaster HRT restricts some content regionally as well.
- **Securing public Wi-Fi** — Adriatic tourist hotspots like Dubrovnik, Split, and Hvar rely heavily on shared hotel and cafe networks during peak summer season. These open networks are prime targets for credential interception, especially in crowded tourist zones.
- **Avoiding ISP throttling** — Hrvatski Telekom and A1 Hrvatska have been documented throttling streaming traffic during peak evening hours in coastal cities where summer tourism strains network capacity. A VPN prevents your ISP from identifying and deprioritizing specific traffic types.

## Best VPN Protocols for Croatia

WireGuard is the recommended protocol for Croatia, delivering the best balance of speed and security on both fiber and mobile connections. OpenVPN (UDP) serves as a reliable fallback, particularly useful when connecting from older hotel network hardware that occasionally interferes with WireGuard handshakes on the Adriatic islands.
