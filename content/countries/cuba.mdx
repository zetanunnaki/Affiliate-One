## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Cuba scores **13 out of 100** on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it among the most repressive internet environments in the Western Hemisphere. The state maintains near-total control over telecommunications through ETECSA, the sole provider of all internet and phone services, while systematically censoring independent media, monitoring citizens' online activity, and imposing shutdowns during protests.

ETECSA operates as a government monopoly with no competition whatsoever. The company launched mobile 3G in 2018 and 4G in 2023 — transforming Cuba from one of the least-connected nations to one with widespread mobile internet access. However, this access comes with severe constraints: data costs are astronomical relative to Cuban salaries ($7 USD for 1GB when average state wages hover around $30/month), speeds are extremely slow (1-5 Mbps typical), and all traffic passes through government-controlled infrastructure. Nauta Hogar provides home broadband in limited areas, while public Wi-Fi hotspots in parks and plazas require ETECSA accounts registered with national ID.

The government's surveillance apparatus monitors all ETECSA traffic. During the July 2021 protests — the largest in decades — authorities imposed a multi-day social media blackout, blocking Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, Telegram, and messaging platforms to prevent protest coordination. Independent journalists and activists report targeted internet disconnections and SIM deactivation. The 2019 Constitution includes provisions protecting "socialist morality" online, and Decree 370 restricts content deemed contrary to the "social interest."

**WARNING: While VPN use is not explicitly criminalized, Cuba's legal framework gives authorities broad discretion to punish circumvention of state controls. Foreigners and citizens face different risk levels — assess your situation carefully.**

## VPN Legality

Cuba has no specific law banning VPNs, but the legal environment is hostile to circumvention. Decree 370 (2019) prohibits disseminating information "contrary to social interest, morals, good customs, and the integrity of people" and can be broadly interpreted. The government has periodically blocked VPN services through ETECSA, and possession of tools designed to circumvent state censorship could theoretically be prosecuted under existing laws. The practical risk for foreign visitors is lower than for Cuban citizens, but caution remains essential.

## Why You Need a VPN in Cuba

Despite the risks, VPN access serves vital functions in Cuba:

- **Access information during shutdowns** — When ETECSA blocks social media during political events, a pre-configured VPN with obfuscation may maintain access to communication platforms and independent news sources
- **Protect against ETECSA surveillance** — All internet traffic passes through a single state monopoly with no privacy protections; VPN encryption prevents authorities from monitoring your browsing, messaging, and communications
- **Reach blocked independent media** — Cuban independent outlets like 14ymedio, CubaNet, and ADN Cuba are blocked domestically; a VPN is the only route to uncensored information about conditions in Cuba

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Cuba's extremely limited bandwidth makes VPN performance a genuine challenge. ETECSA mobile data delivers 1-5 Mbps under normal conditions, and public Wi-Fi hotspots are often slower during peak hours. Through a VPN, expect 0.5-3 Mbps — enough for messaging and basic browsing but frustrating for video calls or streaming. The nearest server locations are in Mexico (Cancun/Mexico City, 50-80ms latency), the United States (Miami, 60-90ms), and Panama (70-100ms).

Miami servers offer the best connectivity for accessing US services, but latency combined with Cuba's slow base speeds means every byte of overhead matters. Download-heavy activities are impractical. Focus on lightweight protocols that minimize data consumption. Wi-Fi hotspots in Havana's parks (Parque Central, Hotel Nacional area) tend to offer slightly better speeds than mobile data for VPN connections.

## Best VPN Protocols for Cuba

Cuba's combination of extreme bandwidth limitations and active censorship demands specific protocol choices. WireGuard's minimal overhead is essential when every kilobit counts — it adds less data consumption than OpenVPN, critical when paying $7/GB. During active blocking periods, obfuscated protocols disguise VPN traffic but add overhead that is painfully noticeable on 1-3 Mbps connections. Psiphon (free, specifically designed for censorship circumvention) serves as a valuable backup that uses multiple techniques to bypass ETECSA filtering with minimal bandwidth cost.
