## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

The country has a well-documented history of complete internet shutdowns and social media blocks during political crises, protests, and transitions of power. The telecommunications market is served by Orange Guinea (the market leader), MTN Guinea, and Cellcom Guinea. Internet penetration remains below 30%, with connectivity concentrated in Conakry and a handful of secondary cities. ## VPN Legality

VPNs are legal in Guinea. No specific laws prohibit their installation or use. However, during government-ordered internet shutdowns, authorities have directed ISPs to block VPN traffic alongside general connectivity restrictions. VPN usage spikes dramatically during these events as citizens attempt to maintain communication.

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No VPN providers maintain servers inside Guinea, so connections route to Senegal (Dakar), Ivory Coast (Abidjan), or France (Paris). Orange Guinea 4G in Conakry typically delivers 5-15 Mbps, with VPN connections to Dakar adding 40-60ms latency. Connections to Paris add 100-140ms but provide access to larger server pools and better peering. During normal operations, these speeds support basic web browsing, messaging, and low-resolution streaming. During partial shutdowns where some traffic leaks through, VPN speeds drop substantially as ISPs throttle all encrypted connections.

## Best VPN Protocols for Guinea

Under normal conditions, WireGuard and OpenVPN (UDP) work reliably in Guinea. During shutdown events, obfuscated protocols are necessary to bypass ISP-level blocking. NordVPN's obfuscated servers, Surfshark's Camouflage Mode, and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol can disguise VPN traffic as standard HTTPS. Users should pre-install VPN apps and download manual configuration files before any anticipated political events, as access to VPN provider websites is typically blocked first.
