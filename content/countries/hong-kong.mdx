## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Hong Kong scores 30/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as Partly Free. This represents a dramatic decline from previous years, driven almost entirely by the 2020 National Security Law which granted authorities sweeping powers to restrict online speech, compel data disclosure, and block websites deemed threatening to national security. Self-censorship among journalists, activists, and ordinary citizens has become widespread, fundamentally altering the territory's once-vibrant digital culture.

Local ISPs such as HKT/PCCW, 3 Hong Kong, SmarTone, and China Mobile Hong Kong are subject to compliance requests under the NSL. Authorities can compel ISPs to block websites and provide subscriber data without traditional judicial oversight. While Hong Kong does not yet operate a mainland-style Great Firewall, the legal framework now allows targeted censorship that would have been unthinkable before 2020.

Given the close relationship between Hong Kong and mainland Chinese intelligence services, privacy-conscious users should assume that internet traffic can be monitored. The lack of a formal data retention mandate does not preclude intelligence gathering under national security provisions. In 2023 and 2024, authorities issued arrest warrants for overseas activists based partly on online activities, and several pro-democracy news outlets including Apple Daily and Stand News were forced to shut down. The Article 23 legislation passed in March 2024 further expanded offenses related to sedition and state secrets, introducing penalties of up to life imprisonment for broadly defined acts including online publication. These developments have pushed many professionals in finance, legal, and media sectors toward encrypted communications and VPN usage as standard operational security.

## VPN Legality

VPN use remains legal in Hong Kong. Unlike mainland China, there is no explicit ban on VPN technology, and VPN provider websites remain accessible. However, the NSL creates legal ambiguity around using VPNs to access content that authorities deem to violate national security provisions. In practice, millions of Hong Kong residents use VPNs daily without legal consequences, and international businesses rely on VPN connections for secure communications. The risk lies primarily with individuals using VPNs for political activism rather than general privacy.

## Why You Need a VPN in Hong Kong

The post-NSL environment has made VPN adoption essential for anyone who values privacy or needs unrestricted internet access in Hong Kong.

- **Accessing geo-restricted content** — Stream US Netflix, BBC iPlayer, or access services that have geo-blocked Hong Kong IP ranges due to licensing changes. Access Japanese streaming platforms like AbemaTV and TVer that are popular among Hong Kong users.
- **Securing public Wi-Fi** — The free Wi-Fi.HK network covers MTR stations, malls, and public spaces but offers zero encryption. With millions of daily users, these networks are prime targets for packet sniffing and man-in-the-middle attacks.
- **Avoiding ISP monitoring** — Under the NSL, ISP data can be requisitioned by authorities. A VPN prevents your ISP from logging which websites you visit and what content you access.
- **Bypassing censorship** — Pro-democracy websites, some news outlets, and certain social media content have been blocked under NSL provisions. A VPN allows access to the full uncensored internet.

## Best VPN Protocols for Hong Kong

Unlike mainland China, Hong Kong does not currently deploy deep packet inspection to block VPN protocols. Standard WireGuard connections work reliably and deliver the best performance. However, given the evolving political situation, having obfuscation capability configured provides future-proofing.

NordVPN's NordLynx (WireGuard-based) delivers the fastest speeds. For users particularly concerned about privacy, NordVPN's obfuscated servers and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol provide an additional layer of protection by disguising VPN traffic as regular HTTPS. If you travel frequently between Hong Kong and mainland China, configure obfuscated protocols before crossing the border, as you will need them on the mainland.
