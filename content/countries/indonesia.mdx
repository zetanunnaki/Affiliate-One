## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Indonesia scores **47/100** on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it in the "Partly Free" category with significant concerns around content blocking, social media throttling during political unrest, and broad government authority over digital communications. The Ministry of Communication and Information Technology (Kominfo) maintains one of the world's most extensive website blocklists, with hundreds of thousands of URLs blocked across all ISPs. Major providers including Telkomsel (IndiHome for fixed broadband), XL Axiata, Indosat, and Biznet are required to enforce these blocks under penalty of license revocation.

Indonesia's fixed broadband infrastructure varies dramatically by location. In Bali and Jakarta, IndiHome (Telkom Indonesia) and Biznet offer fiber connections from 50-300 Mbps at 300,000-600,000 IDR per month. Mobile coverage from Telkomsel, XL Axiata, and Indosat Ooredoo provides 4G connectivity across most of Java and major population centers, with 5G expanding in Jakarta. However, the government demonstrated willingness to throttle social media during the 2019 Papua protests (deliberately slowing WhatsApp, Instagram, and Facebook) and briefly blocked Steam and other gaming platforms in 2022 over registration compliance. The Electronic Information and Transactions Law (UU ITE) grants authorities broad powers over digital content and communications, while the 2022 Personal Data Protection Law (UU PDP) represents a positive step but has enforcement mechanisms still under development.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is legal in Indonesia. Kominfo has not enacted legislation prohibiting VPN services, and they are widely used by Indonesia's tech-savvy population and massive expat community. Indonesia consistently ranks among the highest VPN-usage countries globally in industry surveys. The government has never attempted to block VPN services themselves, and VPN use is openly discussed in Indonesian media and technology forums. There is no gray area — while illegal activities remain illegal regardless of VPN use, the act of using a VPN carries no legal risk in Indonesia.

## Why You Need a VPN in Indonesia

- **Extensive website blocking** — Kominfo maintains a blocklist of hundreds of thousands of URLs including Reddit (blocked since 2014), adult content, gambling sites, and occasionally streaming and gaming platforms, enforced across all Indonesian ISPs
- **Social media throttling** — The government has demonstrated willingness to deliberately slow or block social media during political unrest, as seen during the 2019 Papua protests when WhatsApp, Instagram, and Facebook were throttled to prevent information spread
- **UU ITE surveillance powers** — The Electronic Information and Transactions Law grants authorities broad powers to monitor communications, compel ISPs to provide user data, and order content removal without the judicial oversight standards found in many democracies
- **Unpredictable platform access** — The 2022 Steam blocking incident demonstrated that even mainstream platforms can be restricted with minimal notice over regulatory compliance disputes, making a VPN essential for maintaining reliable access to work tools
- **Public Wi-Fi vulnerability** — Bali's cafes (Crate Cafe, Machinery, Dojo Bali co-working), Jakarta's malls and co-working spaces, hotel networks, and airport Wi-Fi are all shared unencrypted connections vulnerable to interception on networks used by thousands of users daily

## Best VPN Protocols for Indonesia

Indonesia uses DNS-based and IP-based blocking rather than deep packet inspection, meaning standard VPN protocols work reliably without obfuscation. WireGuard-based protocols deliver the best results on Indonesian connections, where bandwidth is often limited and protocol overhead directly impacts usable speed. NordLynx achieved the highest throughput on both IndiHome fiber and Telkomsel mobile networks. OpenVPN (UDP) works reliably but adds noticeable overhead on connections below 100 Mbps — common outside Jakarta and Bali. IKEv2/IPsec excels on Indonesian mobile networks (Telkomsel, XL, Indosat) where tower handoffs are frequent in archipelago environments. For users on remote islands with limited connectivity (Nusa Tenggara, Sulawesi, or Kalimantan), WireGuard's minimal overhead is essential for maintaining usable speeds. During periods of government-ordered social media throttling, having multiple protocol options provides fallback reliability.
