## Italy: Ancient History, Modern Connectivity

Italy is investing heavily in broadband expansion, with fiber reaching most major cities and 5G rolling out nationwide. The country's digital nomad visa (introduced 2024) and affordable living outside Milan and Rome make it increasingly attractive for remote workers. The government's Italia a 1 Giga plan aims to bring gigabit connectivity to the entire country by 2026, and Open Fiber's network expansion is transforming internet access even in smaller towns across Tuscany, Puglia, and Sicily.

## Italian Privacy and Internet Landscape

Italy's data protection authority (Garante per la protezione dei dati personali) is one of Europe's most active GDPR enforcers. Italy was the first country to temporarily ban ChatGPT over privacy concerns (2023), demonstrating aggressive privacy enforcement that extends beyond typical corporate data collection. The Garante has also issued major fines against Clearview AI, telemarketing companies, and government agencies that mishandled citizen data.

Italian ISPs are required to comply with court-ordered content blocking, primarily targeting piracy and gambling sites. The AGCOM authority maintains a blacklist of over 10,000 domains. Italian law also requires ISPs to retain connection metadata for up to six years under anti-terrorism legislation — one of the longest retention periods in the EU. A VPN prevents your ISP from associating this metadata with your specific browsing activity.

## Remote Work in Italy

- **Milan:** Italy's business and tech capital with excellent co-working infrastructure (Talent Garden, Copernico, WeWork) and fiber broadband averaging 500 Mbps to 1 Gbps
- **Rome:** Growing startup scene with beautiful working environments. Many cafes in the historic center offer Wi-Fi, though speeds in older buildings can be inconsistent
- **Florence and Bologna:** University cities with strong digital infrastructure and affordable living. Bologna's tech corridor hosts several major companies
- **Southern Italy and Islands:** More affordable but internet quality can be variable in smaller towns. Fiber rollout is catching up rapidly in cities like Bari, Catania, and Palermo
- **Public Wi-Fi risk:** Italian train stations (Trenitalia and Italo lounges), airports (Fiumicino, Malpensa), and piazzas offer free Wi-Fi. All are shared and unencrypted — a VPN is essential

## Travelers and Digital Nomads

Italy's digital nomad visa allows remote workers earning at least 28,000 euros annually to reside for up to one year, renewable. The visa has attracted thousands of applicants, many of whom work from cafes and co-working spaces on shared networks. Italian SIM cards from TIM, Vodafone, and WindTre offer affordable mobile data (10-20 euros/month for 100-200 GB), but mobile networks are just as susceptible to monitoring as fixed broadband.
