## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Jordan scores 34 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as "Partly Free." Jordan occupies a unique position in the Middle East — more open than the Gulf states, Egypt, or Syria, but increasingly restrictive compared to its own past. VoIP services generally function (unlike UAE or Morocco), and content filtering is less aggressive than regional neighbors. However, the government has expanded its Cybercrime Law to prosecute online speech, and journalists have faced arrest for social media posts. The intelligence services (GID) maintain surveillance capabilities over internet traffic.

The telecommunications market is served by three competitive operators: Orange Jordan (fixed and mobile), Zain Jordan, and Umniah (Batelco Group). These provide 4G and expanding 5G coverage in Amman, Aqaba, and major cities. Orange's fiber network delivers 50-150 Mbps in Amman's commercial districts, while 4G provides 15-40 Mbps across covered areas. Jordan's connectivity routes through submarine cables in the Red Sea (Aqaba) and terrestrial links to neighboring countries, providing adequate international routing with 60-90 ms latency to European hubs.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is legal in Jordan. Unlike many Middle Eastern neighbors, Jordan has not enacted legislation restricting VPN software or encryption tools. VPNs are used openly by businesses, international organizations, and the growing tech community in Amman. The regulatory environment does not target VPN users, though using encrypted tools to conduct activities violating the Cybercrime Law remains prosecutable.

## Why You Need a VPN in Jordan

Jordan's combination of growing cybercrime enforcement, intelligence surveillance, and regional instability creates specific VPN use cases.

- **Protect against Cybercrime Law overreach** — Jordan's broadly worded cybercrime legislation has been used to prosecute journalists, activists, and ordinary citizens for social media posts; VPN encryption prevents ISP-level monitoring of online activities and protects against surveillance that precedes prosecution
- **Secure connections for the growing tech sector** — Amman's tech incubators (The Tank, Zinc, Oasis500) and startup ecosystem rely on shared workspaces and cafe networks; VPN encryption protects intellectual property and client data traversing shared infrastructure
- **Maintain access during regional instability** — Jordan's geographic position between conflict zones means the government has imposed temporary restrictions during security events; VPN provides insurance against unexpected access disruptions

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No major VPN providers operate physical servers in Jordan. The nearest server locations are in Israel (limited availability), Turkey (Istanbul), UAE (Dubai), and Cyprus (Nicosia). Jordan's routing typically passes through European transit points, with Turkey and Cyprus providing the best latency at 40-60 ms. UAE servers deliver 30-50 ms via direct links. European servers (Frankfurt, Amsterdam) offer 70-100 ms latency. With Amman's fiber baseline of 50-150 Mbps, VPN connections through nearby servers retain 70-85% of base speed. On 4G (15-40 Mbps baseline), VPN overhead is manageable for all standard activities including video calls and streaming.

## Best VPN Protocols for Jordan

WireGuard is the recommended protocol for Jordan, delivering optimal performance on the country's moderate bandwidth connections. Jordan does not currently deploy DPI-based VPN blocking, so standard protocols work reliably. OpenVPN over UDP provides a stable alternative for corporate requirements. Given the GID's surveillance capabilities, ensuring strong encryption (AES-256 or ChaCha20) is more important than obfuscation. However, maintaining an obfuscated profile provides insurance against potential restriction escalation during regional security events.
