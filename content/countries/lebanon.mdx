## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Lebanon scores **50 out of 100** on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it at the boundary between "Partly Free" and "Not Free." The country's internet environment reflects its broader crisis — crumbling infrastructure, political fragmentation, and increasing pressure on online expression. While Lebanon historically enjoyed relatively open internet by regional standards, conditions have deteriorated sharply since the 2019 economic collapse.

OGERO, the state-owned fixed-line operator, manages Lebanon's internet backbone and has struggled to maintain infrastructure amid chronic electricity shortages and fuel crises. Power outages lasting 20+ hours daily in many areas mean that internet access itself is intermittent. Mobile operators Alfa and Touch (both state-owned) provide 4G coverage in urban areas, but quality has declined significantly. Private broadband providers like Cyberia and IDM serve parts of Beirut and Mount Lebanon, offering more reliable connections where generator power is available.

The General Directorate of General Security and the Internal Security Forces' cybercrime bureau monitor online activity, with particular attention to content critical of political leaders, religious figures, or the military. The 2012 defamation laws and various press regulations have been applied to online expression. VoIP services including WhatsApp calls were historically blocked to protect telecom revenues, though enforcement has become inconsistent during the crisis.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is legal in Lebanon. There are no specific laws prohibiting VPN services or encryption tools. Given the country's complex political landscape and multiple security agencies, a VPN provides practical privacy protection without legal risk.

## Why You Need a VPN in Lebanon

Lebanon's crisis makes a VPN both a privacy tool and a communication lifeline:

- **Ensure VoIP access** — While enforcement varies, VoIP restrictions can resurface; a VPN guarantees access to WhatsApp calls, Zoom, and other communication tools essential when international calls are prohibitively expensive
- **Route around infrastructure failures** — VPN connections to stable international servers can provide more reliable routing than Lebanon's degraded domestic network, reducing packet loss and connection drops
- **Protect financial privacy** — With the banking crisis ongoing and informal currency markets in play, encrypting financial communications and transactions protects against surveillance by any of Lebanon's multiple security agencies

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Lebanon's infrastructure crisis means realistic expectations are essential. Fixed broadband through OGERO typically delivers 2-10 Mbps when power is available, while private ISPs with generator backup can reach 20-50 Mbps in parts of Beirut. Mobile 4G averages 5-15 Mbps in cities. The nearest VPN server locations are in Cyprus (30-40ms latency), Turkey (40-60ms), and the UAE (60-80ms).

On a typical 10 Mbps OGERO connection, expect 6-8 Mbps through a VPN connected to Cyprus. During generator-powered periods with better ISPs, 15-30 Mbps is achievable. The key challenge is connection stability rather than raw speed — Lebanon's infrastructure produces frequent disconnections that require VPN auto-reconnect features.

## Best VPN Protocols for Lebanon

Given Lebanon's bandwidth constraints and connection instability, WireGuard's lightweight design is ideal — it reconnects faster after network interruptions and adds minimal overhead to already-limited speeds. IKEv2 is a strong alternative for mobile users, as it handles network switches (between Wi-Fi and cellular) seamlessly — critical when power outages force constant switching between connections. Avoid OpenVPN TCP in Lebanon, as its heavier overhead is noticeable on slow connections.
