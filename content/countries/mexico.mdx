## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Mexico scores **52/100** on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it in the "Partly Free" category. While Mexico does not engage in widespread technical censorship, the country faces severe challenges to digital freedom including government surveillance of journalists and activists, violence against media workers, and a legal framework that enables broad data collection. Major ISPs including Telmex (Infinitum), Totalplay, Izzi, and Megacable operate in a market still dominated by Carlos Slim's America Movil, though fiber competition is expanding rapidly in major cities with plans offering 200-500 Mbps for 500-900 MXN per month.

Mexico's Federal Telecommunications Institute (IFT) oversees the sector under the Federal Telecommunications and Broadcasting Law (LFTR), which includes net neutrality provisions but also grants authorities broad access to telecommunications metadata. The National Intelligence Center (CNI) and state-level prosecutors have documented access to surveillance tools, and the Pegasus spyware scandal revealed extensive use of commercial surveillance technology against journalists, human rights defenders, and political figures. ISPs retain connection metadata under telecommunications regulations, and this data is accessible to law enforcement agencies at both federal and state levels.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is completely legal in Mexico with no restrictions. There is no legislation that prohibits, limits, or requires registration of VPN services. Mexican law does not distinguish between encrypted and unencrypted internet traffic. Both individuals and businesses use VPNs freely, and the government has never proposed restricting VPN access. There are no gray areas in enforcement — VPNs are a standard privacy tool used by millions of Mexicans and the large expat and digital nomad community.

## Why You Need a VPN in Mexico

- **Pegasus-level surveillance** — Mexico was one of the most prolific users of NSO Group's Pegasus spyware, deployed against journalists, activists, and political figures, demonstrating government willingness to conduct invasive digital surveillance at the highest technical level
- **Public Wi-Fi risks** — OXXO convenience stores (20,000+ locations), Starbucks, shopping malls, airports (AICM, Cancun, Guadalajara), and hotels offer open Wi-Fi networks that are prime targets for man-in-the-middle attacks and credential theft
- **Elevated cybercrime** — Mexico has high rates of online fraud, phishing, and banking trojans targeting Mexican financial institutions, making VPN encryption essential on any shared network
- **ISP metadata retention** — Telecommunications providers retain connection data accessible to federal and state law enforcement, creating records that link your identity to your online activity
- **ISP throttling** — Despite net neutrality provisions, user reports document Telmex and other ISPs throttling streaming, VoIP, and gaming traffic during peak hours, which VPN encryption prevents by hiding traffic type

## Best VPN Protocols for Mexico

Mexico does not employ deep packet inspection or VPN blocking, so protocol selection focuses on maximizing speed and reliability. WireGuard-based protocols (NordLynx, native WireGuard) deliver the best performance on fiber connections from Totalplay and Izzi. OpenVPN (UDP) provides compatibility with older routers and corporate network configurations common in Mexican businesses. For mobile users on Telcel, AT&T Mexico, or Movistar networks, IKEv2/IPsec offers fast reconnection when moving between cellular towers and Wi-Fi — particularly important in Mexico City where network handoffs are frequent. On slower Telmex DSL connections, lightweight protocols like WireGuard minimize the speed impact that makes VPN use impractical on already-limited bandwidth.
