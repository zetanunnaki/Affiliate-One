## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Morocco scores 37 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as "Partly Free." The government maintains significant control over online speech, with journalists and activists facing prosecution for social media posts critical of the monarchy or territorial integrity. The country's three major telecommunications operators — Maroc Telecom (partially state-owned), Orange Maroc, and Inwi — implement government-directed VoIP blocking that affects millions of users daily. Morocco's broadband infrastructure is reasonably developed, with ADSL and fiber available in major cities delivering speeds of 20-100 Mbps in Casablanca, Rabat, and Marrakech. The ANRT regulator oversees the sector but VoIP restrictions persist as a revenue-protection measure for carriers.

Morocco's surveillance capabilities have drawn international attention following revelations about the use of NSO Group's Pegasus spyware against journalists, activists, and political figures. This sophisticated monitoring infrastructure means that everyday internet traffic traversing Moroccan ISPs is subject to potential inspection, making encryption tools particularly valuable for privacy-conscious users.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is legal in Morocco. There are no laws specifically prohibiting VPN software, and millions of Moroccans use VPNs daily to circumvent VoIP restrictions. The government has not moved to ban VPN services despite their widespread use as workarounds for telecom restrictions. However, using a VPN to conduct activities that are otherwise illegal remains prosecutable.

## Why You Need a VPN in Morocco

Morocco's combination of VoIP blocking, surveillance capabilities, and content restrictions makes a VPN practically essential for visitors and residents.

- **Restore VoIP services** — Maroc Telecom, Orange, and Inwi block WhatsApp calls, FaceTime, Skype, and most VoIP platforms to protect voice call revenue; a VPN is the only reliable workaround used by millions of Moroccans and the entire digital nomad community
- **Protect against Pegasus-class surveillance** — Morocco's documented use of sophisticated spyware means encrypted VPN tunnels provide a critical baseline privacy layer, especially for journalists, researchers, and anyone discussing sensitive political topics
- **Secure digital nomad work connections** — Marrakech, Essaouira, Tangier, and Casablanca host growing remote work communities relying on cafe and riad Wi-Fi networks that lack proper encryption

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Morocco benefits from strong connectivity to European servers due to submarine cables crossing the Strait of Gibraltar and running along the Atlantic coast. The nearest VPN server locations include Spain (Madrid, Barcelona), France (Paris, Marseille), and Portugal (Lisbon). Expect latency of 20-35 ms to Spanish servers and 30-50 ms to French nodes — excellent for real-time VoIP calls. With Morocco's urban broadband baseline of 20-100 Mbps, VPN connections through Iberian servers typically retain 75-90% of base speed. The critical metric for most Moroccan VPN users is VoIP quality, and nearby European servers deliver consistent sub-50ms latency that supports crystal-clear WhatsApp and FaceTime calls.

## Best VPN Protocols for Morocco

WireGuard delivers the best performance for Morocco's primary use case — VoIP calls that demand low latency and minimal jitter. Morocco's VoIP blocking operates at the application layer rather than through deep packet inspection of VPN traffic, meaning standard VPN protocols work reliably without needing obfuscation. OpenVPN on TCP port 443 serves as a backup for restrictive hotel or corporate networks. For users concerned about the country's advanced surveillance capabilities, running VPN traffic through obfuscated tunnels adds an extra privacy layer.
