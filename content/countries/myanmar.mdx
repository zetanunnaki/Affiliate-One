## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Myanmar scores a catastrophic **8 out of 100** on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, making it one of the least free internet environments in the world. Since the February 2021 military coup, the junta has systematically dismantled online freedoms — imposing extended internet shutdowns, blocking virtually all social media, ordering ISPs to install surveillance equipment, and criminalizing VPN use.

The telecommunications market operates under military control. MPT (state-owned), Telenor Myanmar (sold to Lebanese M1 Group under military pressure), Ooredoo Myanmar, and Mytel (military-linked) serve as the primary operators. The junta has ordered all operators to install intercept equipment and block designated services. Facebook — which was Myanmar's primary internet platform, used by over 20 million people — was blocked immediately after the coup, along with Twitter, Instagram, and WhatsApp. Independent news organizations including Myanmar Now, Mizzima, and Democratic Voice of Burma have been blocked entirely.

Complete internet shutdowns have been imposed in conflict areas and during political events, sometimes lasting weeks. The junta has also imposed nightly internet curfews in some regions. Average mobile speeds of 5-15 Mbps on 4G have declined further as infrastructure degrades under sanctions and conflict. Fixed broadband through providers like 5BB and Frontiir serves limited urban areas at 10-30 Mbps.

**WARNING: The military junta has made VPN use illegal. People have been arrested and detained for using VPNs. The information below is for informational purposes — anyone in Myanmar must assess their own risk.**

## VPN Legality

The military junta criminalized VPN use through directives issued to telecom operators after the coup. The 2021 Cyber Security Law draft included provisions explicitly banning VPN services. Arrests have been reported for VPN use, possession of VPN applications on phones, and sharing VPN configurations. The legal penalty can include imprisonment. This represents one of the most dangerous legal environments for VPN use globally.

## Why You Need a VPN in Myanmar

Despite the extreme risks, millions of Myanmar citizens use VPNs because the alternative is complete information blackout:

- **Access any social media** — Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, WhatsApp, and Telegram are all blocked; VPN is the only route to communicate with family, coordinate mutual aid, and access information about the ongoing conflict
- **Reach independent news** — All independent Myanmar media is blocked domestically; without a VPN, citizens only have access to military-controlled state media propaganda
- **Protect against junta surveillance** — The military monitors communications to identify dissidents and protest organizers; VPN encryption provides a critical layer of protection against intercept equipment installed on all operators

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Myanmar's degraded infrastructure severely limits VPN performance. Mobile 4G (5-15 Mbps typical) yields 3-10 Mbps through a VPN under normal conditions, dropping further during active blocking periods when obfuscation is required. Fixed broadband in Yangon (10-30 Mbps) provides slightly better throughput at 7-20 Mbps. The nearest server locations are in Thailand (Bangkok, 30-50ms), Singapore (50-70ms), and India (60-80ms).

Thai servers provide the best performance for everyday use. During crackdown periods, the junta's blocking infrastructure becomes more aggressive, and servers in less commonly blocked locations (Japan, South Korea) may offer better reliability despite higher latency. Connection attempts may fail repeatedly before finding a working server, making apps with auto-connect and server rotation features essential.

## Best VPN Protocols for Myanmar

Standard VPN protocols are actively blocked in Myanmar. Obfuscation is mandatory, not optional. NordVPN's obfuscated servers (XOR-patched OpenVPN) and Surfshark's Camouflage mode are the most reliable commercial options. For backup, configure Shadowsocks (V2Ray plugin), which disguises traffic as normal HTTPS and is harder for Myanmar's less sophisticated DPI to detect compared to China's GFW. Always have multiple protocol configurations ready — what works one week may be blocked the next.
