## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Peru scores 64 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as "Partly Free." The country enjoys a relatively open internet without systematic government censorship, though concerns exist around surveillance legislation and political pressure on media. Peru's telecommunications market is competitive, with Movistar (Telefonica), Claro (America Movil), Entel, and Bitel providing mobile and fixed broadband services. Lima's infrastructure is well-developed with fiber connections delivering 100-300 Mbps in metropolitan neighborhoods like Miraflores, Barranco, and San Isidro.

Outside Lima, connectivity drops significantly. Cusco — a major tourist and digital nomad destination — offers 20-50 Mbps in the city center but coverage weakens rapidly in the Sacred Valley and surrounding areas. Remote regions like the Amazon basin (Iquitos) and highland communities have minimal connectivity, sometimes limited to satellite links. Peru's primary international connectivity runs through submarine cables on the Pacific coast, providing good routing to the United States and excellent connectivity to other South American hubs.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is fully legal in Peru. There are no restrictions on VPN software, and both individuals and businesses use VPNs freely for privacy and remote work purposes. Peru's telecommunications law does not address VPN technology specifically, and there is no political movement toward restriction.

## Why You Need a VPN in Peru

Peru's open internet environment means VPN needs focus primarily on security and access rather than censorship circumvention.

- **Secure public Wi-Fi across tourist circuits** — Cusco's cafes, Lima's co-working spaces, and Sacred Valley hotels all offer shared Wi-Fi networks that expose financial transactions (especially Yape mobile payments and international banking) to interception without VPN encryption
- **Maintain low-latency connections to US employers** — Lima's growing remote work community serves primarily US-based companies; VPN routing through optimized US servers can actually improve connection quality during peak congestion on Peruvian ISPs
- **Access geo-restricted streaming and banking** — Peruvian IP addresses are often blocked by US and European streaming platforms, while travelers abroad lose access to local services like Yape, BCP banking apps, and Latina TV content

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Several VPN providers operate servers in Peru (Lima) or nearby in Brazil (Sao Paulo), Chile (Santiago), and the United States (Miami, Los Angeles). Lima-based servers deliver sub-10ms latency for local users, while Miami servers — critical for US-based remote work — provide 80-100 ms latency via Pacific submarine cables. Santiago servers offer 40-60 ms latency. With Lima's baseline of 100-300 Mbps, VPN connections retain 80-90% of base speed through local or Miami servers. From Cusco (20-50 Mbps baseline), expect 70-80% retention. The key performance consideration for remote workers is choosing US server locations that minimize latency to their employer's infrastructure.

## Best VPN Protocols for Peru

WireGuard is the optimal protocol for Peru across all use cases. The country does not employ VPN blocking or deep packet inspection, so the fastest protocol is always the best choice. WireGuard's lightweight overhead is particularly beneficial for users in Cusco and other areas with moderate bandwidth, where every megabit counts. OpenVPN remains a solid alternative for corporate environments requiring TCP compatibility with strict firewalls. No obfuscation is needed in Peru's regulatory environment.
