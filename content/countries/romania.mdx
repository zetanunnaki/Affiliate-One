## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Romania is not individually assessed by Freedom House's Freedom on the Net report, but the country maintains a generally free internet environment within the EU framework. Romania's Constitutional Court struck down the country's implementation of the EU Data Retention Directive in 2014 (Decision No. 440/2014), declaring mandatory blanket data retention unconstitutional. This landmark decision means Romania has one of the weaker data retention regimes in the EU, though targeted retention for specific investigations remains permissible.

Local ISPs such as Digi (RCS & RDS), Orange Romania, and Vodafone Romania operate under the oversight of ANCOM (National Authority for Management and Regulation in Communications). Following the Constitutional Court's 2014 decision, ISPs are not required to conduct blanket metadata retention. However, Romania's intelligence service (SRI) has been documented using targeted surveillance capabilities, and the country's participation in NATO intelligence frameworks means data can be shared with allied services.

Romania hosts the NATO Aegis Ashore missile defense facility at Deveselu and the Mihail Kogalniceanu Air Base, both of which involve signals intelligence infrastructure. The SRI's cooperation agreements with the NSA and CIA, documented through leaked diplomatic cables and Romanian parliamentary inquiries, indicate that Romanian internet traffic passing through major exchange points in Bucharest may be subject to allied intelligence collection. Additionally, Romania's 2024 cybersecurity legislation expanded DNSC (National Cyber Security Directorate) authority to mandate real-time traffic monitoring on critical infrastructure networks, blurring the line between cybersecurity and surveillance.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are fully legal in Romania. The country's strong constitutional privacy protections, reinforced by the 2014 Constitutional Court ruling against mass data retention, support the right to use encrypted communications tools. There are no licensing requirements for VPN services, and individuals face no restrictions on VPN usage for any purpose.

## Why You Need a VPN in Romania

Despite Romania's favorable legal framework for privacy, practical threats remain. The SRI's targeted surveillance capabilities, Romania's cooperation with NATO and EU intelligence-sharing mechanisms, and the sheer prevalence of public Wi-Fi networks across Bucharest and Cluj-Napoca's tech hubs create compelling reasons for VPN usage.

- **Accessing geo-restricted content** — Romanian services like Pro TV Plus, Antena Play, and Digi Online are geo-locked when traveling abroad, while US and UK streaming libraries remain restricted on Romanian IP addresses
- **Securing public Wi-Fi** — Bucharest's extensive cafe culture (Origo, Seneca Anticafe, The Institute), Cluj-Napoca's tech hub co-working spaces, and Romania's widespread free municipal Wi-Fi networks are all shared and unencrypted
- **Avoiding ISP throttling** — Despite Romania's extraordinary baseline speeds, Orange Romania and Vodafone have been documented applying traffic management to peer-to-peer and streaming traffic on congested urban nodes during peak hours
- **Protecting gaming traffic** — Romania's competitive gaming community benefits from VPN routing optimization that can reduce ping to Western European game servers by avoiding congested peering points between Romanian and Western ISPs
- **Secure remote work for IT sector** — Cluj-Napoca and Bucharest host major outsourcing operations for Fortune 500 companies, and Romanian IT workers handling sensitive client data benefit from VPN encryption that supplements corporate security protocols on home networks

## Best VPN Protocols for Romania

WireGuard is the clear protocol choice for Romania, where the country's gigabit-class infrastructure demands a lightweight protocol to avoid bottlenecking these extraordinary speeds. OpenVPN's higher overhead would leave hundreds of megabits of performance on the table. NordLynx (NordVPN's WireGuard implementation) achieved the closest-to-native speeds in our testing. For the rare cases requiring obfuscation, OpenVPN TCP on port 443 provides adequate fallback without triggering any ISP intervention.
