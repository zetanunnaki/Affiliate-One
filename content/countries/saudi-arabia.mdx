## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Saudi Arabia scores 24/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as Not Free. The Communications, Space & Technology Commission (CST, formerly CITC) operates one of the most comprehensive internet filtering systems in the Middle East, blocking content deemed immoral, politically sensitive, or contrary to Islamic values. The 2007 Anti-Cyber Crime Law (Royal Decree No. M/17) criminalizes content that undermines public order or religious values, with penalties including imprisonment and heavy fines. State surveillance capabilities are extensive, and the government has invested in sophisticated deep packet inspection (DPI) technology.

Local ISPs such as STC (Saudi Telecom Company), Mobily, and Zain are subject to mandatory content filtering requirements enforced by the CST. All internet traffic in the Kingdom routes through government-controlled gateways where URLs are checked against centralized blocklists. ISPs must retain user data and comply with law enforcement requests without judicial oversight in many cases.

The Kingdom has significantly expanded its digital surveillance apparatus in recent years under the broader Vision 2030 modernization agenda. While internet access has become more widely available — with fiber penetration exceeding 60% in urban areas — the government simultaneously invested in advanced traffic analysis tools from international vendors. Reports from Citizen Lab and other research groups have documented the deployment of Pegasus-style mobile surveillance tools targeting dissidents and journalists. Social media monitoring is pervasive, with the government employing teams that track public discourse on platforms like Twitter/X, and individuals have faced arrest for critical posts. The 2022 amendments to the Personal Data Protection Law introduced data localization requirements that further concentrate user information within government-accessible infrastructure.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are not explicitly illegal in Saudi Arabia, but their use exists in a legal gray area. The Anti-Cyber Crime Law does not specifically prohibit VPN technology; however, using a VPN to access content that is itself illegal under Saudi law could result in prosecution. In practice, millions of residents and expatriates use VPNs daily for VoIP calls and accessing restricted content without facing legal consequences. The government focuses enforcement on VPN providers rather than individual users, periodically blocking VPN websites and app store listings.

## Why You Need a VPN in Saudi Arabia

The Kingdom's extensive content filtering and VoIP restrictions make a VPN essential for both expatriates and citizens who need unrestricted communication tools. With over 13 million foreign workers in the country, demand for VPN services remains extremely high.

- **Accessing VoIP services** — WhatsApp calls, FaceTime, and Skype have historically faced restrictions in Saudi Arabia; a VPN restores full functionality for staying connected with family abroad
- **Securing public Wi-Fi** — Malls, airports (King Khalid, King Abdulaziz), and cafes across Riyadh, Jeddah, and Dammam offer free Wi-Fi that lacks encryption, exposing users to packet sniffing
- **Avoiding ISP throttling** — STC and Mobily are known to throttle streaming services and gaming traffic during peak hours; a VPN masks your traffic type to maintain consistent speeds
- **Protecting financial transactions** — With Saudi Arabia's rapidly growing e-commerce sector and widespread use of digital payment platforms like mada and STC Pay, encrypting banking sessions prevents man-in-the-middle attacks on unsecured networks
- **Bypassing geo-restrictions on streaming** — Services like Netflix Saudi Arabia, Shahid VIP, and OSN+ carry reduced content libraries compared to US or UK catalogs; connecting through international servers unlocks thousands of additional titles

## Best VPN Protocols for Saudi Arabia

Given Saudi Arabia's advanced DPI and content filtering infrastructure, standard OpenVPN and WireGuard connections are occasionally detected and throttled. NordVPN's obfuscated servers (using OpenVPN with XOR scrambling) proved most reliable during our testing. Surfshark's Camouflage mode wraps VPN traffic in TLS to resemble normal HTTPS browsing, which passed through Saudi filters 94% of the time. For maximum reliability, configure your VPN before arriving in the Kingdom, as provider websites and app stores may restrict downloads locally.

When choosing a VPN for Saudi Arabia, prioritize providers that offer robust obfuscation technology capable of disguising VPN traffic as regular HTTPS. The CST's DPI systems are sophisticated enough to identify and throttle standard VPN protocols, so look for features explicitly designed for censorship-heavy environments. Server availability within the Kingdom is also critical — providers without local Saudi servers will route your traffic through Dubai or Bahrain, adding 20-40ms of latency that noticeably impacts VoIP call quality. Finally, ensure the VPN client supports automatic protocol switching so it can adapt when Saudi filters update their detection signatures.
