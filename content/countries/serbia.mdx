## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Serbia scores 66 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as "Partly Free." Serbia enjoys relatively open internet access without systematic content blocking, but concerns exist around government surveillance, pressure on independent media, and the growing use of facial recognition and mass surveillance technology. Under the ruling SNS party, pro-government tabloids dominate the media landscape, while independent online outlets face harassment and occasional DDoS attacks. The country's intelligence services have expanded digital surveillance capabilities with limited judicial oversight.

The telecommunications market is competitive, with SBB (United Group), MTS (Telekom Srbija, state-controlled), and Yettel (formerly Telenor) providing mobile and fixed broadband. Belgrade's infrastructure is excellent — fiber connections deliver 100-500 Mbps widely available for under 20 EUR/month, making Serbia one of Europe's best value propositions for internet access. Novi Sad, Nis, and other regional cities also have strong coverage. Serbia's position along European terrestrial fiber routes provides excellent international connectivity with low latency to Western European hubs.

## VPN Legality

VPN use is fully legal in Serbia. There are no restrictions on VPN software or encryption tools. The country's tech-savvy workforce and growing IT sector mean VPNs are standard tools for developers, remote workers, and privacy-conscious users. Serbia's regulatory framework does not address VPN technology, and there is no political momentum toward restriction.

## Why You Need a VPN in Serbia

Serbia's open internet makes VPN needs focus on privacy, professional requirements, and content access rather than censorship circumvention.

- **Protect against expanding state surveillance** — Serbia's intelligence services have deployed facial recognition and mass surveillance infrastructure with limited oversight; VPN encryption prevents ISP-level traffic monitoring, particularly important given MTS (Telekom Srbija) is state-controlled and could facilitate surveillance
- **Secure public Wi-Fi across Belgrade's cafe culture** — Belgrade's legendary kafana and cafe scene offers free Wi-Fi on shared, unencrypted networks; tech workers and digital nomads using these spaces for work need VPN protection for corporate data and personal banking
- **Access geo-restricted content while traveling** — Serbian IP addresses are blocked by many international streaming platforms, while Serbians traveling abroad lose access to RTS, N1, and local services; a VPN solves both directions of geo-restriction

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

Several VPN providers operate servers in Serbia (Belgrade), providing local performance. NordVPN and others maintain Serbian server infrastructure delivering sub-5ms latency for local users. Nearby alternatives include servers in Romania (Bucharest), Hungary (Budapest), and Austria (Vienna) with 15-30 ms latency. Given Serbia's excellent baseline of 100-500 Mbps on fiber, VPN connections through local servers retain 90-95% of base speed — effectively transparent on most connections. International VPN servers in Germany (Frankfurt) and Netherlands (Amsterdam) deliver 25-40 ms latency, making Serbia one of the better-connected VPN environments in the Balkans.

## Best VPN Protocols for Serbia

WireGuard is the clear optimal protocol for Serbia, taking full advantage of the country's excellent bandwidth to deliver near-native speeds. Serbia does not employ any VPN blocking, DPI, or protocol restriction, making the fastest protocol always the best choice. The high baseline speeds mean even OpenVPN UDP performs well here, though WireGuard's lower overhead provides measurably better throughput on the available fiber connections. No obfuscation is needed in Serbia's regulatory environment.
