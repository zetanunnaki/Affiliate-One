## Spain: Sun, Siestas, and Strong Internet

Spain has become one of Europe's top digital nomad destinations, driven by its digital nomad visa (introduced 2023), affordable cost of living outside major cities, and excellent quality of life. Barcelona, Valencia, the Canary Islands, and Malaga are global remote work hotspots attracting tens of thousands of international workers who need reliable, secure internet connections.

## The Spanish Digital Nomad Visa

Spain's Law 28/2022 created a specific visa for remote workers. Key requirements include working for a non-Spanish company and earning above the Spanish minimum wage. The visa is valid for up to 5 years and offers tax advantages under the Beckham Law for the first 6 years — allowing qualifying workers to pay a flat 24% income tax rate instead of the progressive Spanish scale. This has attracted thousands of international remote workers, all of whom need secure internet connections on shared networks.

## Internet Infrastructure

- **Movistar, Vodafone, Orange, MasMovil:** Competitive fiber market with speeds up to 1 Gbps widely available in cities for 30-50 euros per month. Spain has one of the highest fiber penetration rates in Europe, with over 85% of households having access to fiber
- **Canary Islands:** Excellent infrastructure for an island region. Las Palmas de Gran Canaria has some of Spain's best co-working spaces (Restation, The House) and a year-round nomad community
- **Barcelona:** World-class digital infrastructure, hundreds of co-working spaces (MOB, Aticco, OneCoWork), major tech hub with Mobile World Congress
- **Valencia:** Growing tech scene with more affordable pricing than Barcelona. The Ruzafa and El Carmen neighborhoods are packed with nomad-friendly cafes

## Privacy and Security Considerations

Spain's data protection authority (AEPD) actively enforces GDPR and has issued some of the highest fine volumes in Europe. However, Spanish ISPs retain connection metadata under the Ley de Conservacion de Datos, and Spain's National Intelligence Centre (CNI) has surveillance capabilities that extend to digital communications. The 2023 Pegasus spyware scandal — where the Spanish government was found to have used NSO Group's spyware against Catalan politicians — underscored the importance of encryption tools for anyone operating in the Spanish digital space.

Public Wi-Fi at Renfe train stations, airports (El Prat, Barajas, Malaga-Costa del Sol), and beach towns is universally open and unencrypted. A VPN is essential for protecting sensitive work data and personal banking on these networks.
