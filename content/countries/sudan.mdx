## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Sudan scores 23 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, placing it in the "Not Free" category. The civil war between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) that erupted in April 2023 has devastated the country's telecommunications infrastructure and triggered extended internet shutdowns across multiple states. Zain Sudan, MTN Sudan, and Sudatel are the primary operators, though service in conflict zones has been repeatedly destroyed or deliberately disabled by warring factions. Before the conflict, internet penetration was approximately 30%, concentrated in Khartoum, Port Sudan, and other major cities. The war has reduced effective connectivity dramatically, with entire regions experiencing months-long blackouts. Both warring parties weaponize internet shutdowns to control information flow during military operations.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are not formally banned in Sudan, though the government has historically blocked VPN services during political crises. Under the former al-Bashir regime, VPN websites were blocked and usage was discouraged. Current conflict conditions have made enforcement essentially impossible in many areas, and VPN usage is widespread among those who maintain connectivity. Aid workers, journalists, and diaspora-connected Sudanese routinely rely on VPNs.

## Why You Need a VPN in Sudan

In Sudan's active conflict environment, a VPN is critical for both information access and personal security.

- **Survive deliberate shutdowns** — Both SAF and RSF forces order regional internet cuts during military operations; when partial connectivity exists, VPN tunnels can bypass protocol-specific blocks and social media restrictions
- **Protect against wartime surveillance** — Both warring factions monitor communications to identify perceived enemies; VPN encryption prevents interception of messages that could lead to targeting
- **Access blocked humanitarian information** — International news, human rights documentation, and humanitarian coordination platforms face blocking; VPN connections maintain access to critical information during the crisis

## Server Coverage & Speed Performance

No VPN provider operates servers within Sudan. The nearest locations are in Egypt (Cairo), Saudi Arabia (Jeddah), and the UAE (Dubai). Sudan's international connectivity routes primarily through undersea cables at Port Sudan on the Red Sea coast. Where mobile networks remain functional, baseline speeds average 2-8 Mbps on 4G, though service interruptions are constant in conflict areas. VPN connections to Egyptian servers add 40-80ms latency with 25-40% throughput reduction. Saudi and UAE servers offer 60-100ms latency. During partial shutdowns, effective VPN speeds may drop to sub-1 Mbps — functional for messaging and basic browsing only. Port Sudan and eastern cities generally maintain better connectivity than Khartoum and western regions.

## Best VPN Protocols for Sudan

Both warring factions employ DPI and VPN blocking when controlling telecommunications infrastructure. Standard protocols face intermittent blocking, particularly during military operations. Obfuscated connections are essential. NordVPN's obfuscated servers, Surfshark's Camouflage Mode, and Proton VPN's Stealth protocol disguise VPN traffic as regular HTTPS, bypassing blocks deployed by both SAF and RSF-aligned operators. Users must pre-install multiple VPN apps and download manual configuration files while connectivity is available, as access to VPN websites is unpredictable.
