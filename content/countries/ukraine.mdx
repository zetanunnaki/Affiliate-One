## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

Ukraine scores 55/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as Partly Free. The ongoing conflict has complicated Ukraine's internet freedom landscape significantly. While Ukraine historically maintained a relatively open internet, wartime measures have introduced content restrictions and the government has blocked Russian-owned platforms since 2017 under Presidential Decree No. 133/2017. The State Service of Special Communications and Information Protection (SSSCIP) oversees cybersecurity and has expanded surveillance capabilities under martial law provisions enacted in 2022.

Local ISPs such as Kyivstar, Vodafone Ukraine, and lifecell continue operating across the country but face infrastructure challenges in conflict-affected areas. These operators comply with government data requests under the Law on Telecommunications, which mandates cooperation with security services. Data retention periods have been extended under wartime legislation, and ISPs in government-controlled territory block access to hundreds of Russian websites and services.

The cybersecurity threat landscape in Ukraine is among the most intense globally. CERT-UA has documented thousands of attacks attributed to Russian state-sponsored groups since 2022, including destructive wiper malware (HermeticWiper, CaddyWiper), supply chain compromises targeting Ukrainian software companies, and persistent phishing campaigns against government employees. The December 2023 cyberattack on Kyivstar — which disrupted mobile service for 24 million subscribers for several days — demonstrated the vulnerability of Ukrainian telecommunications infrastructure. Starlink has become a critical backup connectivity option in areas where terrestrial infrastructure has been damaged, but shared satellite terminals in shelters and community centers create unique security challenges where multiple users share a single uplink without individual network segmentation.

## VPN Legality

VPNs are fully legal in Ukraine with no restrictions on personal or commercial use. The Ukrainian government does not restrict encryption tools, and VPN adoption is actively encouraged by cybersecurity authorities for protecting sensitive communications. Many Ukrainian government agencies and military units themselves rely on VPN technology for secure communications across disrupted infrastructure.

## Why You Need a VPN in Ukraine

The conflict has dramatically elevated cybersecurity threats in Ukraine, with state-sponsored Russian hacking groups (Sandworm, Fancy Bear, Gamaredon) conducting persistent campaigns against Ukrainian infrastructure and citizens. VPN usage has become a practical security necessity rather than a privacy luxury.

- **Accessing blocked Russian services** — Millions of Ukrainians still have contacts and content on VKontakte, Yandex, and Mail.ru services blocked since 2017; a VPN provides access when needed for personal reasons
- **Securing public Wi-Fi and Starlink** — Shared Starlink terminals in shelters, co-working spaces in Lviv, and public networks in Kyiv's metro lack individual encryption, making VPN protection critical against interception
- **Avoiding ISP throttling** — Kyivstar and Vodafone UA throttle bandwidth-heavy applications during peak hours to manage limited wartime capacity; VPN encryption prevents traffic classification and preferential degradation
- **Protection against targeted cyberattacks** — Russian APT groups deploy credential-harvesting phishing pages mimicking Ukrainian government services (Diia, Privat24, Monobank); a VPN with built-in threat protection blocks connections to known malicious domains before they load
- **Maintaining access during infrastructure disruptions** — When local DNS servers go down or ISP routing tables are corrupted during attacks, connecting through a VPN bypasses compromised local infrastructure entirely by routing through international DNS and servers

## Best VPN Protocols for Ukraine

Ukraine does not employ censorship-oriented DPI, so the primary protocol consideration is reliability on potentially unstable connections. WireGuard's lightweight design makes it ideal for situations where bandwidth is limited or connections frequently drop, as it re-establishes tunnels faster than OpenVPN. For users on Starlink or mobile connections with variable packet loss, NordLynx (WireGuard-based) provided the best experience during our testing. OpenVPN TCP is recommended as a fallback when UDP-based protocols struggle on degraded infrastructure.

When choosing a VPN for use in Ukraine, connection reliability takes precedence over raw speed. Look for providers that offer automatic kill switches to prevent unencrypted data leaks during the frequent connection drops caused by infrastructure instability. Multi-server failover — where the client automatically switches to a backup server in Poland or Romania if the primary Ukrainian server goes offline — is essential. Lightweight protocols like WireGuard are preferred because they re-establish connections in milliseconds rather than the seconds required by OpenVPN, critical when your internet drops and reconnects multiple times per day.
