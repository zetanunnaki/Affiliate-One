## Internet Freedom & Privacy Landscape

The United States scores 76/100 on Freedom House's Freedom on the Net index, classified as Free. While Americans enjoy open internet access without government-mandated content filtering, the country has significant structural privacy weaknesses. The 2017 repeal of FCC broadband privacy rules (S.J.Res.34) allows ISPs to collect, store, and sell subscriber browsing data without explicit consent. Major ISPs including Comcast (Xfinity), AT&T, Verizon, Charter (Spectrum), and T-Mobile Home Internet are legally permitted to monetize customer internet activity through targeted advertising programs.

The United States operates under a patchwork of federal and state privacy laws rather than a single comprehensive framework like the EU's GDPR. At the federal level, FISA Section 702 authorizes mass surveillance of international communications that transit US infrastructure, and the NSA's upstream collection programs intercept data flowing through major internet exchange points in Ashburn (Virginia), 60 Hudson Street (New York), and One Wilshire (Los Angeles). The Snowden revelations confirmed that domestic internet traffic is routinely swept up in this collection. The USA FREEDOM Act (2015) reformed some bulk collection practices, but intelligence agencies retain broad authority under Executive Order 12333.

At the state level, California's CCPA/CPRA provides the strongest consumer protections, including the right to know what data is collected, the right to delete it, and the right to opt out of its sale. Colorado, Connecticut, Virginia, Utah, Texas, Oregon, and Montana have enacted their own privacy laws with varying protections. However, the majority of US states have no comprehensive privacy legislation, leaving residents reliant on their ISP's voluntary privacy policies. The lack of a federal privacy law means Americans in most states have no legal right to prevent their ISP from building detailed profiles of their online activity.

## VPN Legality

VPN usage is completely legal in the United States with no restrictions. There are no laws prohibiting VPN use for personal privacy, remote work, or accessing content. The US government does not block VPN protocols or provider websites. However, using a VPN to commit illegal acts (fraud, hacking, copyright infringement) remains illegal regardless of the VPN — the tool is legal, but criminal activity conducted through it is not. US-based VPN providers can be compelled to hand over data through court orders, which is why privacy-focused users prefer providers headquartered outside US jurisdiction.

## Why You Need a VPN in the United States

Despite being classified as Free, the US presents unique privacy challenges that make VPN usage essential for security-conscious individuals, remote workers, and families seeking to protect their digital footprint from commercial surveillance.

- **Preventing ISP data monetization** — Comcast, AT&T, Verizon, and other major ISPs legally collect and sell browsing history to advertisers and data brokers, building detailed profiles of every website you visit, search query you make, and service you use
- **Securing remote work connections** — With 35% of US workers operating remotely at least part-time, home networks shared with IoT devices, co-working spaces like WeWork and Regus, and coffee shop Wi-Fi at Starbucks and Panera all present attack surfaces for corporate data
- **Escaping Five Eyes surveillance** — As a founding member of the Five Eyes alliance (with UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand), US intelligence agencies conduct mass internet surveillance through NSA programs; a VPN with a no-logs policy based outside Five Eyes jurisdiction prevents traffic correlation
- **Defeating ISP throttling** — Major US ISPs have been documented throttling Netflix, YouTube, and gaming traffic during peak hours; a VPN prevents your ISP from identifying and selectively slowing specific services
- **Protecting on public Wi-Fi** — Airport networks (Boingo), hotel chains (Marriott, Hilton), public libraries, and municipal Wi-Fi systems are shared, unencrypted, and vulnerable to man-in-the-middle attacks

## Best VPN Protocols for the United States

Since the US does not employ deep packet inspection or VPN blocking, users can freely choose the fastest available protocol without obfuscation concerns. WireGuard (or NordVPN's NordLynx implementation) delivers the best performance for most US users, achieving near-native speeds on fiber and cable connections. OpenVPN UDP remains a solid alternative for users who prefer the battle-tested security of OpenVPN's codebase. IKEv2/IPsec is ideal for mobile users who frequently switch between cellular (5G/LTE) and Wi-Fi networks, as it handles network transitions seamlessly without dropping the VPN tunnel. For corporate environments, OpenVPN TCP on port 443 can bypass restrictive workplace firewalls that block other protocols.
